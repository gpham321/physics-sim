Double-Slit, Single-Slit, & Multi-Slit Interference

When light passes through narrow slits, each slit becomes a Huygens point source. The waves that emerge overlap and interfere — bright bands where crest meets crest, dark bands where crest meets trough. Three demos let you isolate what controls the pattern: the wavelength, the gap between slits, the width of each slit, and the number of slits.

The master formulas

For \(N\) identical slits separated by distance \(d\) (center-to-center) and illuminated by light of wavelength \(\lambda\), bright fringes appear at angles \(\theta\) from the central axis where the path-length difference between adjacent slits is an integer number of wavelengths:

$$ \boxed{\,d\,\sin\theta \;=\; m\,\lambda\,} \qquad m = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots $$

For small angles (\(\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta = y/L\)), the fringes land on a screen a distance \(L\) away at heights \(y_m \approx m\,\lambda L / d\). So the spacing between bright fringes is \(\Delta y = \lambda L / d\).

Each individual slit also diffracts on its own. If a slit has width \(a\), its dark fringes are at:

$$ a\,\sin\theta \;=\; m'\,\lambda \qquad (m' = \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots) $$

The full intensity for \(N\) slits of width \(a\) and spacing \(d\) is the product of the multi-slit interference and the single-slit envelope:

$$ I(\theta) \;=\; I_0 \left[\frac{\sin(N\beta)}{N\,\sin\beta}\right]^2 \left[\frac{\sin\alpha}{\alpha}\right]^2, \quad \beta = \tfrac{\pi d \sin\theta}{\lambda},\quad \alpha = \tfrac{\pi a \sin\theta}{\lambda}. $$

How to use \(d\sin\theta = m\lambda\)

Three flavors of the same equation, depending on what you know and want:

  1. Find a fringe angle. Given d, λ, and order m: \(\theta_m = \sin^{-1}(m\lambda/d)\).
  2. Find a wavelength. Spectroscopy: measure the angle \(\theta_m\) of a known order m of a spectral line: \(\lambda = (d\sin\theta_m)/m\). This is exactly how we measured atomic spectra and discovered quantum mechanics.
  3. Find slit spacing. Given a known wavelength and the measured fringe angle: \(d = m\lambda/\sin\theta_m\).

For small angles (\(\theta < \sim 10°\)) you can use \(\sin\theta \approx \theta \approx y/L\), giving the fringe spacing \(\Delta y = \lambda L/d\).

Worked example

A laser pointer at \(\lambda = 632\) nm shines on a double slit with \(d = 0.1\) mm. The screen is \(L = 2.0\) m away. Where does the \(m = 1\) bright fringe appear?

\(\sin\theta_1 = (1)(6.32\times 10^{-7})/(1.0 \times 10^{-4}) = 6.32 \times 10^{-3}\), so \(\theta_1 \approx 0.36°\). On the screen: \(y_1 \approx (2.0)(6.32 \times 10^{-3}) = 1.26\) cm from center.

What to look for

  • Demo 1: drag wavelength higher (red) and watch the fringes spread out. Drag separation d higher and they squeeze together. The product λ/d controls everything.
  • Demo 2 (single slit): shrink a and the diffraction pattern fans out — narrow slit, wide spread. This is exactly the position-momentum tradeoff (see the Heisenberg sim).
  • Demo 3: raise N from 2 to 5 to 20. The maxima get sharper and the regions between get blacker. This is why diffraction gratings are spectroscopy's go-to tool.
  • Highest visible order: if mλ/d > 1, no real angle works, so the fringe doesn't exist. With small d, only a couple of orders fit at all.
  • Strip ↔ graph alignment: the vertical positions of bright bands on the screen strip exactly match the peaks in the intensity profile to its right. The two views are the same data shown two ways.
