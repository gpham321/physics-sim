Light and Matter, Lasers

Where does light come from? The short answer: electrons jumping between energy levels in atoms — but the how determines whether you get a warm tungsten glow, a sodium-yellow streetlight, a UV-driven fluorescent tube, glow-in-the-dark paint, or a laser. Same physics, very different light.

The three fundamental light-matter processes

Every light source below boils down to combinations of three elementary events between a photon and an atom. Click each one to see what happens.

The photon energy that participates in any of these processes has to match the level gap exactly: $$ E_{\text{photon}} \;=\; h f \;=\; E_2 - E_1. $$ Photons with the wrong energy just fly past — the atom doesn't see them.

Thermal (broadband) vs. line emission

When you heat anything hot enough, its constituents — atoms vibrating, jostling, colliding — emit photons across an enormous range of energies. The result is a broadband blackbody spectrum that depends only on temperature. The Sun, a fire, a glowing iron, an incandescent bulb filament — all the same physics.

When you instead let isolated atoms (a low-pressure gas) drop from one definite quantum level to another, each transition emits a photon at exactly the matching energy gap. The spectrum is a forest of sharp lines, unique to the element. Sodium yellow, neon red, hydrogen Balmer red/blue — all signatures of specific atomic transitions.

Why broadband sources are inefficient. A thermal source dumps most of its energy into infrared (heat you can't see). Only a small slice lands in the visible window (380–780 nm). Line sources can place all their output right in the visible if the transitions are chosen well — that's why fluorescent and LED bulbs use a tenth of the power for the same brightness as an old incandescent.

Lifetimes, metastable states, and linewidth

An excited atom doesn't sit in its upper level forever — it has a lifetime \(\Delta t\) characteristic of that transition. After roughly \(\Delta t\) seconds on average, the electron drops and emits its photon. Typical optical transitions: \(\sim 10\) ns. Some transitions are special: a metastable state has a much longer lifetime (μs to seconds) because the obvious decay is "forbidden" by quantum selection rules.

Metastable states are what make lasers and phosphorescent (glow-in-the-dark) materials work — they let electrons pile up in an upper level before falling.

Heisenberg: lifetime ↔ linewidth

$$ \Delta E \cdot \Delta t \;\geq\; \frac{\hbar}{2}, \qquad \Delta E_{\min} \;=\; \frac{\hbar}{2\,\Delta t}. $$

From Worksheet 14: \(\Delta t = 1\) μs (metastable) → \(\Delta E_{\min} \approx 3\times 10^{-10}\) eV (very narrow). \(\Delta t = 1\) ns → \(3\times 10^{-7}\) eV (1000× broader).

Incandescent — thermal radiation

A tungsten filament heated to ~2700 K glows because any hot object radiates electromagnetic waves — the same physics as the Sun, just at a cooler temperature. Most of the energy comes out as invisible infrared, which is why incandescent bulbs are so inefficient.

Stefan–Boltzmann: total radiated power per unit area $$ P/A \;=\; \sigma T^4, \qquad \sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}\ \text{W/m²/K⁴}. $$ Wien displacement: peak wavelength $$ \lambda_{\max} \;=\; b/T, \qquad b = 2.898 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m·K}. $$

For tungsten at 2700 K: \(\lambda_{\max} \approx 1070\) nm — well into the infrared. The visible window (380–780 nm) catches only the short-wavelength tail.

Why it's fundamentally inefficient. To make the peak land in the visible, you'd need \(T \approx 5800\) K (the Sun's surface). But tungsten melts at 3695 K, so you can't get there without vaporizing the filament. Result: ~5% of input power becomes visible light; the rest is wasted as IR heat.

Discharge lamps — gas atoms emit lines

A tube full of low-pressure gas. Run current through it; the gas atoms get excited by collisions with fast-moving electrons; as they relax they emit photons at specific wavelengths set by the atomic energy levels. Each gas has its own fingerprint spectrum.

  1. Apply high voltage. A few free electrons get accelerated.
  2. Electron-atom collisions. Each fast electron slams into an atom and kicks one of its electrons up to a higher level — "electron-impact excitation."
  3. Avalanche. Some collisions ionize the atom, releasing more free electrons. The discharge sustains itself.
  4. Spontaneous emission. Excited atoms drop down, emitting photons of energy \(hf = E_\text{upper} - E_\text{lower}\). Because levels are discrete, the photons come out as sharp lines.
$$ \lambda \;=\; \frac{hc}{E_{\text{upper}} - E_{\text{lower}}}. $$

Fluorescent — UV cascades to visible

A fluorescent bulb takes one high-energy UV photon and breaks it into multiple visible photons by cascading down through intermediate atomic levels:

  1. Discharge. Voltage drives current through mercury vapor inside the tube.
  2. Mercury emits UV. Excited Hg atoms relax by emitting 254 nm photons (invisible UV, ≈ 4.9 eV).
  3. Phosphor cascades UV → visible. The tube's inner phosphor coating absorbs each UV photon, the electron cascades through intermediate levels, and multiple visible photons come out.
The whole point: convert one invisible high-energy photon into several visible photons. Same total energy, but now where your eye can use it — ~4× more efficient than incandescent.

Decay times and step-downs

Each excited state has its own lifetime \(\Delta t_{i \to j}\). The probability of taking a particular decay path is set by the rate \(1/\Delta t_{i\to j}\) compared to all other rates leaving level \(i\):

$$ P(i \to j) \;=\; \frac{1/\Delta t_{i\to j}}{\sum_{k} 1/\Delta t_{i\to k}}. $$

If \(\Delta t_{3 \to 2} \ll \Delta t_{3 \to 1}\), the atom almost always cascades through E₂. That's the regime fluorescent bulbs are engineered to be in. For multi-level phosphors, the atom marches down step by step in ~ns, total emission practically instantaneous to your eye.

If one of the intermediate levels happens to be metastable, the atom gets stuck for microseconds or longer — that's phosphorescence (next tab).

How engineers control the decay paths

A phosphor's color and efficiency depend entirely on which decay paths it favors. Decay rates aren't fixed by physics alone — they can be engineered. The main levers:

1. Choice of host material and dopant

A phosphor is usually a transparent host crystal lightly doped with a small percentage of an emitting ion. The dopant sets emission energies and lifetimes; the host's role is to be transparent and hold the dopant in a particular geometry.

  • Eu³⁺ (europium): sharp red at 610 nm. Used in red TV phosphors and warm fluorescents.
  • Tb³⁺ (terbium): sharp green near 545 nm.
  • Ce³⁺ in YAG: broad yellow. The phosphor that makes "white" LEDs by absorbing blue.
  • Mn²⁺: green-to-orange depending on host.

2. Selection rules — "allowed" vs. "forbidden" transitions

QM says some transitions are allowed (fast, ns lifetime) and others forbidden (slow, μs–s lifetime). "Forbidden" doesn't mean impossible — it means the transition violates a symmetry rule and proceeds much more slowly. Tune the rules and you tune the decay rate.

$$ \Delta \ell = \pm 1, \qquad \Delta m_\ell = 0, \pm 1, \qquad \Delta s = 0. $$

Transitions that satisfy these (e.g. \(s \to p\), \(p \to d\)) are allowed. Transitions that violate them (any spin flip, \(s \to s\)) are forbidden.

3. The heavy-atom effect

One way to "turn on" a forbidden transition is to introduce a heavy atom nearby. Heavy nuclei have strong spin-orbit coupling that mixes states of different spin — and once states are mixed, even spin-forbidden transitions can happen at a respectable rate. Phosphors deliberately use heavy host ions (Y³⁺, Eu³⁺, La³⁺) for this reason.

Practical consequence: heavy-atom doping shortens lifetimes of normally forbidden transitions, speeding up phosphorescence into ordinary fluorescence. Light-bulb designers use this trick to avoid long afterglow — they want the bulb to turn off when you flip the switch.

4. Crystal field splitting

When an emitting ion sits in a host crystal, the surrounding charges break the spherical symmetry of the free ion. This splits otherwise degenerate levels and shifts their energies. Depends on:

  • Site symmetry — octahedral vs. tetrahedral vs. trigonal coordination.
  • Bond length — closer ligands give stronger splitting.
  • Covalency — electron sharing softens the levels (nephelauxetic effect).

Same ion, different surroundings, different color. Ce³⁺ in YAG glows yellow; in a different garnet it glows green.

5. Phonon coupling (lattice vibrations)

The emitting ion is embedded in a vibrating crystal. Lattice vibrations (phonons) can absorb energy from the excited electron, providing a non-radiative decay pathway. Too much phonon coupling and emission efficiency drops. The competition between radiative decay (photon) and non-radiative decay (phonons) sets the quantum yield — fraction of absorbed photons that produce an emitted photon.

  • Cooling the phosphor → fewer phonons → less non-radiative loss → brighter, longer-lived emission.
  • Choosing a host with low phonon energies → reduces phonon-assisted decay; makes forbidden transitions sharper.

6. Concentration quenching

Doping more emitter ions doesn't just produce more light. Above a critical concentration, neighboring excited ions transfer energy to each other and dissipate it non-radiatively. Phosphor designers find a sweet spot (typically 1–10% dopant) for maximum brightness without quenching.

7. Co-doping for energy transfer

Use two dopants: one that absorbs strongly (the sensitizer) and one that emits at the desired wavelength (the activator). Energy transfers from sensitizer to activator radiationlessly, then the activator emits. Decouples absorption color from emission color — that's how "tri-phosphor" fluorescent tubes hit specific R/G/B ratios.

Real example. A modern "cool white" fluorescent uses a tri-phosphor blend: Eu³⁺:Y₂O₃ (red 611 nm), Tb³⁺:CeMgAl₁₁O₁₉ (green 543 nm), Eu²⁺:BaMgAl₁₀O₁₇ (blue 450 nm). Each absorbs 254 nm Hg UV, cascades, and emits its assigned narrow line. The three peaks look "white" to your eye.

Phosphorescent — fluorescence with one slow step

Same physics as fluorescence (UV absorbed, cascades to visible) — but one of the intermediate levels is metastable. Electrons get trapped there for seconds, minutes, even hours before they finally decay. That's why a glow-in-the-dark sticker keeps shining long after the lights go out.

A metastable state is one where the obvious downward transition is spin-forbidden. The selection rule \(\Delta s = 0\) makes the transition 10⁵–10¹⁰ times slower than the corresponding allowed transition.

Typical lifetimes:
  • Fluorescent (allowed): \(\tau \sim 10^{-9}\) s (1 ns)
  • Phosphorescent (forbidden metastable): \(\tau \sim 10^{-3}\) s to \(10^4\) s (1 ms to 3 hours)

Lasers — light amplification by stimulated emission

A laser takes one photon, copies it, copies the copies, and spits out millions of identical photons — same frequency, same phase, same direction. The trick is forcing stimulated emission to win over absorption, and that takes a clever multi-level scheme.

Absorption

Photon disappears; electron up. Rate \(\propto N_1\).

Spontaneous emission

Excited atom decays on its own. Random direction/phase. Rate \(\propto N_2/\tau\).

Stimulated emission

Passing photon clones itself off an excited atom. Same direction/phase. Rate \(\propto N_2\) × flux.

Einstein 1917 showed that absorption and stimulated emission share identical rate coefficients. They differ only in which level the electron starts in. That symmetry is the central problem the laser has to overcome.

Why a two-level laser is impossible

Blast a two-level atom with photons at \(hf = E_2 - E_1\). Each photon is equally likely to be absorbed (driving an atom up) or to stimulate emission (driving an atom down):

$$ \text{Net stimulated emission} \;\propto\; (N_2 - N_1) \cdot \text{(photon flux)}. $$

Pump harder and \(N_2\) rises — but as soon as \(N_2 = N_1\), absorption and stimulated emission balance. You cannot get past 50/50.

Conclusion (Worksheet 14, problem 3): No matter how brightly Eve pumps a 2-level system, she will never get \(N_2 > N_1\). The pump that excites atoms also stimulates them right back down. Two-level lasers don't work.

The three-level fix (Ruby laser, 1960)

Add a third level above the lasing transition. Pump runs between \(E_1\) and the high \(E_3\), not between the lasing levels. Atoms in \(E_3\) decay quickly to the upper lasing level \(E_2\) — and stay there, because \(E_2 \to E_1\) is metastable.

The four steps:

  1. Pump. Shine photons of \(f_{\text{pump}} = (E_3 - E_1)/h\). Atoms absorb, jumping \(E_1 \to E_3\).
  2. Fast non-radiative \(E_3 \to E_2\). High level dumps energy into vibrations in picoseconds. Atoms accumulate in \(E_2\).
  3. Population inversion built. \(E_2 \to E_1\) is slow (metastable, μs–ms), so atoms pile up faster than they leave.
  4. Lasing! Any stray photon of \(f_{\text{laser}} = (E_2 - E_1)/h\) triggers a cascade of stimulated emission.

The four-level scheme — even better

A 3-level laser's lower lasing level is the ground state. To get inversion you need to depopulate more than half of all ground-state atoms — that takes serious pumping. A 4-level scheme adds a fourth level just above ground:

  1. Pump \(E_1 \to E_4\) (top).
  2. Fast non-radiative \(E_4 \to E_3\) (metastable upper lasing).
  3. Lase on \(E_3 \to E_2\). Lower lasing level \(E_2\) starts empty.
  4. Fast non-radiative \(E_2 \to E_1\) keeps \(E_2\) empty.

Because \(E_2\) is empty, any atom in \(E_3\) creates inversion immediately. Much lower pump-power threshold. Nd:YAG (1064 nm) and He-Ne (633 nm) are 4-level. The ruby laser (3-level) needs an intense flashlamp; He-Ne lases continuously on milliwatts.

The optical cavity — why a laser is a beam, not a glow

Stimulated emission alone gives amplification, but for a coherent beam you need feedback. Two parallel mirrors bounce the photons back and forth through the gain medium, picking up more clones with each pass. One mirror is fully reflective; the output coupler is partially transmissive — a beam leaks out.

Cavity modes

For a standing wave between mirrors at separation \(L\), the cavity must equal an integer number of half-wavelengths:

$$ L = \frac{m\lambda}{2}, \qquad m = 1, 2, 3, \dots $$

This selects discrete modes. Lasing happens at cavity modes that fall within the gain medium's linewidth. Fewer modes = purer laser line.

Coherent light vs. everything else

Stimulated emission produces photons identical to the trigger. After many cavity round trips, the beam is a vast number of phase-locked photons marching in step. A flashlight, by contrast, runs on spontaneous emission — each atom decays independently, giving a tangled mess of waves.

Why coherence matters:
  • Tight beam: coherent waves don't spread; focus to a near-diffraction-limited spot 100 m away.
  • Monochromatic: single frequency → spectroscopy, atomic clocks, metrology.
  • High intensity: all energy in one beam, not spread 4π steradians.
  • Interference is usable: holograms, interferometers, LIGO.

Linewidth ↔ pulse duration

From Worksheet 14: \(\Delta E_{\min} = \hbar / (2 \Delta t)\). A short-lived emission has a broad line; a long-lived (metastable) emission has a sharp one.

  • CW laser: long emission time → razor-sharp line. Stabilized He-Ne: \(\Delta f \sim 1\) kHz.
  • Femtosecond pulse: short emission → deliberately broad spectrum. 10 fs Ti:sapphire: \(\Delta f \sim 50\) THz, spans hundreds of nm.

Why photons can do this at all — bosons vs fermions

From Worksheet 14 problem 9: stimulated emission piles many photons into the same quantum state. Photons are bosons (spin 1) — any number can share one state. Electrons (spin ½) are fermions; Pauli exclusion forbids them from sharing a state.

If photons were fermions, you literally could not build a laser — only one photon would fit in each cavity mode. Bose–Einstein statistics is the deep reason stimulated emission cascades the way it does: adding a photon to an already-occupied mode is statistically favored for bosons.

Where lasers are everywhere

  • Communications: 1550 nm fiber pulses carry every bit of the internet.
  • Manufacturing: CO₂ and fiber lasers cut metal, weld cars, etch boards.
  • Medicine: LASIK (excimer), tumor ablation, dermatology.
  • Sensing: LIDAR, gravitational-wave detection (LIGO is a 4 km laser interferometer), atomic clocks.
  • Storage and display: Blu-ray (blue), DVD (red), holography, projectors.
  • Fundamental research: Atom cooling, attosecond pulses, inertial-confinement fusion (NIF: 192 lasers).

LED — recombination across a semiconductor band gap

A p-n junction: one region (n-type) has extra electrons, the other (p-type) has extra "holes" (missing electrons in the valence band). Apply forward bias and electrons flow into the junction, drop into holes, and release the energy difference as photons.

$$ hf \;=\; E_g \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \lambda \;=\; \frac{hc}{E_g}. $$

Band-gap energy directly sets the color:

  • InGaAs (\(E_g \approx 0.7\) eV) — IR, 1500 nm
  • GaAs (\(E_g \approx 1.4\) eV) — NIR, 870 nm
  • AlGaInP (\(E_g \approx 1.9\) eV) — red, 650 nm
  • GaP (\(E_g \approx 2.2\) eV) — yellow-green, 565 nm
  • InGaN (\(E_g \approx 2.7\) eV) — blue, 460 nm
  • AlGaN (\(E_g \approx 3.4\) eV) — UV, 365 nm

Why LEDs are so efficient

  • No thermal waste. The chip isn't hot — it's just shuttling electrons across a gap. Almost all energy becomes photons (~40–60% wall-plug vs ~5% for incandescent).
  • Narrow band emission. Every recombination drops the same \(E_g\), so the spectrum is a ~20 nm-wide band right at the design wavelength.
  • Long lifetime. No filament to burn, no Hg to leak. 25,000–50,000 hours typical.

How do you make white from one band gap?

  1. Phosphor-converted. Blue (or UV) LED + yellow phosphor (Ce:YAG). Phosphor absorbs some blue, re-emits broad yellow via fluorescence. Blue + yellow ≈ white. Dominant type.
  2. RGB mix. Three LEDs (red, green, blue). Better color rendering, more expensive. Used in displays.
Nobel 2014: Akasaki, Amano, Nakamura. Red and green LEDs existed for decades, but blue LEDs needed InGaN, cracked in the 1990s. With blue LEDs, the phosphor trick suddenly worked and white-LED bulbs became possible.

Quick rule: is a source broadband or lines?

Every source on the previous tabs is one of two types. Broadband means the light contains a continuous range of wavelengths — like a sweep of every colour. Line means the light is concentrated at a few specific wavelengths, leaving the rest dark. The distinction tells you what's making the light:

Broadband ⇔ atoms aren't isolated. Either they're packed together (hot solid, hot dense gas) and bumping into each other so often that their energy levels smear out, or they're constrained to a band of states (semiconductor). Either way, the emission covers a continuous range. Examples: incandescent, LED (narrow but still a band).

Lines ⇔ atoms are isolated. A low-pressure gas, a solid-state phosphor where each ion sits alone in a host, or a single atomic transition in a laser. Each electron drops a definite energy gap and emits a sharp wavelength. Examples: discharge, fluorescent (sharp lines broadened slightly by the phosphor host), phosphorescent, laser.

Decision tree — "what type of source is this?"

Split white light with a prism (or look at it through a diffraction grating, like a CD reflection). What do you see?

  1. Smooth full rainbow (every colour present continuously) → broadband thermal source (incandescent, the Sun, fire, a glowing piece of iron). If the rainbow looks balanced and warm-yellow-tinted, ~2700 K incandescent. Bluer → hotter.
  2. A few bright lines on a black background → line source (discharge / fluorescent / phosphorescent).
    • Three obvious peaks (blue + green + red) on otherwise dark spectrum → tri-phosphor fluorescent.
    • One sharp doublet at 589 nm and almost nothing else → low-pressure sodium.
    • Cluster of red/orange lines → neon discharge.
    • Visible-region lines plus invisible UV → mercury vapour.
    • Visible lines that persist after lights out → phosphorescent.
  3. One single pure wavelength, beam stays tight at long distances → laser.
  4. One narrow band ~20 nm wide, single colour but a band not a line → LED.

The deep reason behind the rule

An atom in isolation has a discrete set of energy levels, so its emission spectrum is a set of sharp lines — one per transition. Bring many atoms together at high density and several things smear those lines out:

  • Pressure broadening. Atoms collide so often that each one's energy levels are perturbed by neighbours during emission. Each line gets wider.
  • Doppler broadening. Thermal motion shifts the emitted wavelength up or down depending on atom velocity. Lines widen proportional to \(\sqrt{T/m}\).
  • Coupling to neighbours. In a solid, electron states aren't isolated — they overlap with neighbours to form continuous bands. This is the whole story behind metals and semiconductors.

So the spectrum tells you about the matter:

Hot dense matter (filament, dense plasma like the Sun's photosphere, lava) → all of the above happen at once → smooth broadband.

Thin, isolated atoms (low-pressure gas tube, a star's tenuous outer atmosphere, an excited dopant ion in a glass host) → minimal broadening → sharp lines.

Semiconductors → electrons sit in bands of allowed states, not discrete atomic levels → narrow-band emission instead of either lines or a full thermal sweep.

That's why looking at a light source's spectrum tells you almost everything you need to know about how it generates light.
