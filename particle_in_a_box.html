Particle in a Box

A quantum particle locked between two infinitely tall walls. The simplest place where energy quantization, wavefunctions, and probability distributions all show up at once — and where you can actually see why nature is built out of standing waves.

What is the wavefunction?

In quantum mechanics, a particle isn't a tiny dot at a definite location. It's described by a wavefunction \(\psi(x)\) — a wave-like mathematical object that's spread out over space.

The wavefunction itself isn't directly observable. What you observe is a probability:

$$ P(\text{particle in a small region around } x) \;=\; |\psi(x)|^2 \,\Delta x. $$

So the square of the wavefunction, \(|\psi(x)|^2\), is a probability density — like a "cloud" telling you where the particle is likely to be found if you were to measure its position. Tall regions = likely; near-zero regions = unlikely. The total area under \(|\psi|^2\) over all of space equals 1, because the particle has to be somewhere.

Two pictures, one object. Below you'll see two graphs side by side: the wavefunction \(\psi(x)\) (which can be positive or negative, like an ordinary wave) and \(|\psi(x)|^2\) (always positive — it's a probability density). The first is the wave; the second is what you can actually measure.

How does \(\psi\) tell you about momentum and energy?

Here is the key bridge between waves and particles, due to Louis de Broglie (1924):

$$ p \;=\; \frac{h}{\lambda}, \qquad E_{\text{kin}} \;=\; \frac{p^2}{2m}. $$

Read this as: look at the wavelength \(\lambda\) of the matter wave, and you've already determined the particle's momentum. Short wavelength = wiggly wave = high momentum. Long wavelength = slow wiggle = low momentum. Then once you have \(p\), the kinetic energy is the usual \(p^2/(2m)\).

So a wavefunction is doing two jobs at once:

  • Its square tells you where the particle is.
  • Its wavelength tells you how fast (momentum) it's moving, and therefore its energy.

Now we just need a setup where the wave is forced to fit nicely — and that's the box.

Why energy comes in steps: the standing-wave argument

Consider a particle (say, an electron) trapped in a 1-dimensional box of length \(L\). The walls are infinitely tall, so the particle can never escape — the wavefunction must drop to zero exactly at \(x=0\) and at \(x=L\). This is identical to the boundary condition for a guitar string fixed at both ends.

A guitar string fixed at both ends only supports certain wavelengths — those that fit a whole number of half-wavelengths into the length:

$$ n\frac{\lambda_n}{2} \;=\; L \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \lambda_n \;=\; \frac{2L}{n}, \qquad n=1,2,3,\dots $$

Plug this into de Broglie: \(p_n = h/\lambda_n = nh/(2L)\). And then \(E_n = p_n^2/(2m)\):

$$ \boxed{\;E_n \;=\; \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2}, \qquad p_n \;=\; \frac{nh}{2L}, \qquad \lambda_n \;=\; \frac{2L}{n}.\;} $$

The wavefunctions themselves are sine waves that vanish at both walls:

$$ \psi_n(x) \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\,\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \quad\text{for } 0
Three big takeaways.
  • Quantization is automatic. Only certain wavelengths fit, so only certain energies are allowed. Quantum numbers \(n=1,2,3,\dots\) drop out of geometry, not by hand.
  • Energy grows as \(n^2\). Going from \(n=1\) to \(n=2\) quadruples the energy; \(n=3\) means \(9\times\) the ground-state energy.
  • The ground state has nonzero energy. The minimum is \(E_1 = h^2/(8mL^2)\), not zero. A confined quantum particle must be moving — that's the uncertainty principle.

2-D box — two quantum numbers

Two dimensions = two boundary conditions = two quantum numbers \((n_x, n_y)\). The wavefunction is just the 1-D answer in \(x\) multiplied by the 1-D answer in \(y\):

$$ \psi_{n_x,n_y}(x,y) \;=\; \frac{2}{\sqrt{L_x L_y}}\,\sin\!\left(\tfrac{n_x \pi x}{L_x}\right)\sin\!\left(\tfrac{n_y \pi y}{L_y}\right). $$

Energies just add too:

$$ E_{n_x,n_y} \;=\; \frac{h^2}{8m}\!\left(\frac{n_x^2}{L_x^2} + \frac{n_y^2}{L_y^2}\right). $$

If \(L_x = L_y\) (a square box) you get something new called degeneracy: states like \((1,2)\) and \((2,1)\) have the same energy but different wavefunctions. Squash the box (\(L_x \neq L_y\)) and the degeneracy lifts.

3-D box — three quantum numbers

Three dimensions = three quantum numbers \((n_x, n_y, n_z)\). Same product structure, same additive energies:

$$ \psi_{n_x,n_y,n_z}(x,y,z) \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{8}{L_x L_y L_z}}\,\sin\!\left(\tfrac{n_x \pi x}{L_x}\right)\sin\!\left(\tfrac{n_y \pi y}{L_y}\right)\sin\!\left(\tfrac{n_z \pi z}{L_z}\right). $$ $$ E_{n_x,n_y,n_z} \;=\; \frac{h^2}{8m}\!\left(\frac{n_x^2}{L_x^2} + \frac{n_y^2}{L_y^2} + \frac{n_z^2}{L_z^2}\right). $$

For a cubic box (all sides equal), states like \((1,1,2)\), \((1,2,1)\), \((2,1,1)\) are triply degenerate — same energy, three different wavefunctions. This degeneracy structure is the seed of why elements come in groups in the periodic table.

What the wavefunction lets you compute

The whole point of all these sine waves is that, given \(\psi(x)\), every measurable property of the particle is just a few steps away. For algebra-based intuition (no calculus needed):

  • Where is the particle? Look at \(|\psi(x)|^2\). High values = likely; zeros = forbidden. This is the only thing you can directly measure as a position experiment.
  • Most likely position? The peaks of \(|\psi(x)|^2\). For \(n=1\) in a 1-D box, the peak is at the center; for \(n=2\), the center is forbidden and there are two peaks at \(L/4\) and \(3L/4\); for higher \(n\), \(n\) evenly spaced peaks.
  • Wavelength → momentum. Read the wavelength of the standing wave: \(\lambda_n = 2L/n\). Then \(p_n = h/\lambda_n = nh/(2L)\). The bigger \(n\) is (more wiggles), the bigger \(p\) is.
  • Momentum → kinetic energy. Apply \(E = p^2/(2m)\). For our box this gives \(E_n = n^2 h^2/(8 m L^2)\).
  • Energy gaps → photon spectra. If a particle drops from level \(n\) to level \(n'\), it emits a photon of energy \(E_n - E_{n'}\) and frequency \(f = (E_n - E_{n'})/h\). This is exactly how atomic spectra work, just with a more complicated potential than a flat box.
Why this toy model matters. Real systems aren't perfect boxes, but the box gives the scaling right: confine a particle in a region of size \(L\) and its kinetic energy must be at least of order \(h^2/(mL^2)\). This is why electrons confined to atomic-sized regions (\(L \sim 0.1\) nm) sit at electron-volt energies, why nucleons confined to a nucleus (\(L \sim 1\) fm) sit at MeV energies, and why quantum-dot LEDs glow different colors when you change the dot size. The rule is universal: smaller box, bigger energies, bigger gaps.

Things to try

  • Sit on the 1-D tab and slide \(n\) from 1 up to 10. Count the peaks of \(|\psi|^2\) — there are exactly \(n\) of them. Notice that classically (a ball bouncing back and forth) you'd expect equal probability everywhere, and quantum mechanics matches this for huge \(n\) (the correspondence principle).
  • Halve the box width \(L\). The energy quadruples. The wavelengths halve. Confining things costs energy — period.
  • On the 2-D tab, set \((n_x, n_y) = (2,1)\) and then \((1,2)\). Same energy in a square box, different probability cloud (rotated 90°). Slide the aspect ratio away from 1 — the energies separate.
  • On the 3-D tab, set \((n_x, n_y, n_z) = (2,1,1)\) and then move the slice slider to \(z/L = 0.5\). The slice through the middle has its full \(xy\) structure. Now move the slice toward 0 or 1 — it fades to zero (the \(z=0\) and \(z=L\) walls are nodes for any \(n_z\)).
  • For \((n_x, n_y, n_z) = (1,1,2)\) with the \(xy\)-slice at \(z = 0.5\): the slice is dark! Because \(\sin(2\pi \cdot 0.5) = 0\). Move the slice off-center to see the bump come back.
