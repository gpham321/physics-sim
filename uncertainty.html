Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle

You cannot know both the position and the momentum of a quantum particle to arbitrary precision — sharpening one measurement automatically blurs the other. This isn't an experimental limitation; it's a deep statement about wavefunctions. Two demos let you watch it happen.

The principle

For a quantum particle described by a wavefunction \(\psi(x)\), the standard deviations of position and momentum measurements obey:

$$ \boxed{\;\Delta x \;\Delta p \;\geq\; \frac{\hbar}{2}\;} \qquad \hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi} \approx 1.055 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J\,s} $$

An equivalent textbook form using \(h\) directly:

$$ \Delta x \;\Delta p \;\geq\; \frac{h}{4\pi}, \qquad \Delta E \;\Delta t \;\geq\; \frac{h}{4\pi}. $$

The second inequality says you can't know the energy of a state and the time at which it had that energy to arbitrary precision either — that's why excited atomic states have a "natural linewidth" set by their lifetime: \(\Delta E \approx \hbar/\tau\).

This is not "the act of measurement disturbs the system." Even if you do nothing, the particle has no definite position and momentum simultaneously — it's a statement about what reality is, not about what we can measure.

Demo 1 — Single-slit diffraction

A particle (here a photon) passes through a single narrow slit of width \(a\). After the slit, it could be detected anywhere in a band on a screen — the smaller you make \(a\), the wider the band gets. That widening is the uncertainty principle in action.

Position: the moment the particle clears the slit, you know its vertical position to within \(\Delta x \sim a\).

Momentum: the diffraction pattern's first dark fringe sits at \(\sin\theta = \lambda / a\). Treating the central bright peak as the momentum spread, \(\Delta p_y \sim p \sin\theta = p\,\lambda/a\). For a photon \(p = h/\lambda\), so:

$$ \Delta x\,\Delta p_y \;\sim\; a \cdot \frac{h}{a} \;=\; h. $$

That's an order-of-magnitude estimate, and it lands above the strict bound \(\hbar/2 = h/(4\pi)\) — exactly the spirit of the uncertainty principle.

Drag this all the way left for a tiny slit (very narrow Δx → very wide diffraction → big Δp_y). Drag it right for a wide slit (loose Δx → narrow beam → small Δp_y).
Visible spectrum 380–780 nm. Longer wavelength → larger angular spread for the same slit (so larger Δp_y/p too).

Photon path through the slit

Photons (small dots) travel left-to-right. They get filtered by the slit and then spread according to the diffraction envelope. Smaller slit → larger spread.

Intensity on the screen

Single-slit diffraction pattern \(I(\theta) = I_0 [\sin\alpha/\alpha]^2\), \(\alpha = \pi a \sin\theta/\lambda\). The first dark fringe is at \(\sin\theta = \lambda/a\) — that's the angular spread \(\Delta\theta\) we use to estimate \(\Delta p_y\).

Demo 2 — Gaussian wavepacket: position vs. momentum

A Gaussian wavepacket \(\psi(x) \propto e^{-x^2/(2\sigma_x^2)}\,e^{ik_0 x}\) is the special "minimum-uncertainty" state — it saturates the bound \(\Delta x \cdot \Delta p = \hbar/2\) exactly. Its Fourier transform (the momentum-space wavefunction) is also a Gaussian, with width \(\sigma_p = \hbar/\sigma_x\).

So if you make the position distribution narrow (you "know where the particle is"), the momentum distribution becomes wide (you don't know where it's going) — and vice versa. They are Fourier conjugates.

The Fourier transform — also Gaussian, centered at k₀, with width σ_k = 1/σ_x. Multiply σ_k by ℏ to get σ_p.

For a Gaussian wavepacket, the product \(\sigma_x\sigma_p = \hbar/2\) — they're standard deviations, not full widths, so this is the strict lower bound. Any other shape (square pulse, triangle, decaying exponential…) gives a strictly larger product.

Demo 3 — Excited-state lifetime & spectral linewidth

An atom can sit in an excited state only for a finite time τ before spontaneously emitting a photon. The corresponding energy uncertainty (the "natural linewidth" of the emission) follows from the energy-time uncertainty relation:

$$ \Delta E \cdot \Delta t \;\geq\; \tfrac{\hbar}{2} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \Delta E \;\gtrsim\; \frac{\hbar}{\tau}. $$

Long-lived (metastable) states have very sharp spectral lines. Short-lived states have broad ones. Measure the linewidth → infer the lifetime, and vice versa. This is how nuclear physicists determine half-lives of states they can't follow with a stopwatch (e.g., the Mössbauer effect).

Logarithmic scale: 10⁻² ns (10 ps, very fast) up to 10³ ns (1 μs, long). Real lifetimes range from femtoseconds (autoionizing states) to thousands of years (some nuclear isomers).
The center of the emission line. Hα (Balmer) is 1.89 eV; doesn't change the linewidth, just shifts the curve.

A high \(Q\) means a very pure spectral line — the basis of laser frequency standards and atomic clocks. Cesium's clock transition has Q > 10¹⁵.

How to read this: a typical optical transition (τ ~ 10 ns) has Γ ~ 10⁻⁷ eV — practically invisible to the eye but measurable with a high-resolution spectrometer. Slide τ down to picoseconds (very short) and the line balloons to ~mV — the atom barely "knows" what its energy is.

Other ideas the uncertainty principle explains

  • Why atoms don't collapse. A classical electron should spiral into the nucleus and radiate forever. But confining the electron to a tiny Δx forces a huge Δp, and the resulting kinetic energy outweighs the gain in Coulomb binding energy. Minimizing the total gives the Bohr radius — the atom finds a balance.
  • Zero-point energy. A quantum oscillator in its ground state has nonzero kinetic energy because Δx = 0 would require Δp = ∞. Liquid helium never freezes at atmospheric pressure for the same reason.
  • Tunneling. A particle's energy is uncertain enough on short timescales (ΔE Δt ≈ ℏ) to "borrow" energy and slip through a barrier it classically couldn't surmount. This is what powers nuclear fusion in the Sun and the operation of every flash drive.
  • Cosmological vacuum fluctuations. Even empty space fizzes with particle-antiparticle pairs that pop in and out of existence on borrowed energy — the seeds of structure in the early universe came from inflated quantum fluctuations.
  • Resolution limit of any wave imaging. Microscopes can't resolve features smaller than ~λ/2 (Abbe diffraction limit). Electron microscopes get around it by using electrons with tiny de Broglie wavelengths, made small by giving them large momentum.
