Electron Diffraction — Davisson–Germer & Bragg

If de Broglie's idea is right and electrons have a wavelength \(\lambda = h/p\), then they should diffract off crystal lattices the same way X-rays do. Two experiments — one in reflection (Davisson & Germer, 1927) and one in transmission (Bragg-style, refined by G. P. Thomson) — confirmed it.

The physics

Louis de Broglie proposed in 1924 that any particle has an associated wave with

$$ \lambda \;=\; \frac{h}{p} \;=\; \frac{h}{\sqrt{2 m_e E}} \quad\text{(non-relativistic electron)}.$$

For a 54 eV electron, \(\lambda \approx 1.67\) Å — comparable to atomic spacings in metals. So a crystal lattice acts as a natural diffraction grating for electrons, and the same Bragg condition that works for X-rays should work here:

$$ 2d\,\sin\theta \;=\; n\lambda. $$

The two experiments below test this prediction with different geometries:

  • Davisson & Germer (1927): low-energy electrons scattered off the surface of a single nickel crystal. A movable detector measures intensity as a function of scattering angle. A sharp peak appears at \(\sim 50^\circ\) from the normal — exactly where the Bragg-like surface-grating condition is satisfied.
  • Bragg / G. P. Thomson (1927): high-energy electrons fired through a thin metal foil. Each crystallite acts as a Bragg reflector for whichever orientation matches \(2d\sin\theta = n\lambda\). The result on a screen is a set of concentric rings (powder pattern) — the famous Debye–Scherrer rings, the same ring pattern X-rays produce.

Bragg geometry — interference between atomic planes

Inside a crystal, parallel planes of atoms (separated by lattice spacing \(d\)) act as partial reflectors. Two parallel rays, one reflecting off the top plane and one off the next plane down, interfere. The bottom ray travels an extra \(2d\sin\theta\); when this equals an integer number of de Broglie wavelengths, the reflected beams add constructively.

Snap to Bragg order:

Drag θ or use the snap buttons. Wavelet packets travel along each ray; the "extra" segments on the bottom ray (highlighted gold) sum to the path difference \(2d\sin\theta\). When that equals an integer multiple of λ — orders n = 1, 2, 3, … — the two reflected beams arrive in phase and the diffraction pattern lights up. Anywhere else the two beams interfere destructively and no electrons reach the detector. (Demo uses \(d/\lambda = 2.0\); first three Bragg orders sit at θ ≈ 14.5°, 30°, 48.6°.)

Angular screen — what each model predicts

Imagine putting a long detector strip along the outgoing-beam direction and counting where electrons land. WRONG (classical particles): with no wave nature there's no interference — billiard-ball electrons would scatter in every direction with no preferred angle, so the screen is just a uniform dust of dots. RIGHT (matter waves): dots pile up sharply at the Bragg-condition angles \(2d\sin\theta = n\lambda\) and almost nowhere else. The narrow stripes at 14.5°, 30°, 48.6° are the n = 1, 2, 3 diffraction orders — the same orders the snap buttons jump to.

Why both matter

  • Independent confirmations. Davisson & Germer and Thomson published in the same year (1927) using completely different geometries and energy scales. Both gave the same answer: \(\lambda = h/p\). They shared the 1937 Nobel Prize.
  • The wave nature is intrinsic, not an artifact. If only one experiment had worked, you might suspect the geometry. Two different geometries giving the same \(\lambda\) means electrons really do carry a wavelength.
  • This generalizes. Neutron diffraction, atom interferometry, even big-molecule diffraction (C₆₀, peptides) all obey the same de Broglie relation. The wave nature is a property of matter, not a quirk of electrons.
