Bohr Model — Atomic Energy Levels

Atoms only absorb and emit photons of certain wavelengths. Bohr's idea: an electron lives on quantized circular orbits with energy \(E_n = -13.6\,\text{eV}\,Z^2/n^2\). Tune the laser to a matching wavelength and fire a photon — the electron absorbs it and jumps up. Higher orbits are unstable and the electron will spontaneously drop back down, emitting a photon of just the right color.

The physics

For a hydrogen-like atom (one electron, nuclear charge \(+Ze\)), Bohr's quantization condition \(L = n\hbar\) gives:

$$ E_n \;=\; -13.6\ \text{eV}\ \frac{Z^2}{n^2} \qquad (n = 1, 2, 3, \dots) $$

An electron in level \(n_i\) absorbs a photon and jumps up to level \(n_f > n_i\) only if the photon's energy matches the gap:

$$ hf \;=\; E_{n_f} - E_{n_i} \;=\; 13.6\ \text{eV}\ Z^2 \left(\tfrac{1}{n_i^2} - \tfrac{1}{n_f^2}\right). $$

The reverse process is spontaneous emission: an excited electron drops to a lower level after a finite lifetime and spits out a photon. Higher levels are more unstable (shorter lifetimes), so the electron tends to cascade down through several levels in succession. In wavelength language (with \(\lambda = hc/E\) and \(hc = 1240\) eV·nm):

$$ \frac{1}{\lambda} \;=\; R\,Z^2\!\left(\tfrac{1}{n_f^2} - \tfrac{1}{n_i^2}\right), \qquad R = 1.097\times 10^{7}\ \text{m}^{-1}. $$

For hydrogen (\(Z = 1\)), transitions ending on \(n_f = 1\) are the Lyman series (UV), \(n_f = 2\) are Balmer (visible), \(n_f = 3\) are Paschen (IR).

What to look for

  • Default starts ready for Lyman α. Wavelength is 122 nm, which matches the n: 1 → 2 transition for hydrogen. Hit "Fire one photon" and the electron jumps. Then watch it spontaneously cascade back down.
  • Wrong wavelength → no jump. Slide a few nm off any matched transition and the photon will just streak across the atom and out the other side. The match indicator turns from green back to muted.
  • "Force excite (n+1)" auto-tunes a photon to exactly the n → n+1 transition wavelength and fires it for you. The destination orbit briefly highlights gold so you know where the electron will end up. Click again to walk further up the ladder.
  • "Force emit (n−1)" drops the electron one level immediately, releasing a photon. Useful when you don't want to wait for the random spontaneous-emission timer.
  • Higher levels are more unstable. An electron at n = 2 can sit there for several seconds before dropping; an electron at n = 6 typically lasts only fractions of a second. Watch the cascade — it usually drops one level at a time, with each step emitting a photon of a different color.
  • Z² scaling. Switch to He⁺ — every transition needs 4× the energy (¼ the wavelength). The Lyman α of He⁺ is 30.4 nm — very deep UV, much harder to produce in real life.
