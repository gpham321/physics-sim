Relativistic Velocity Addition

A rocket flies past at 0.6 c and shoots a ball forward at 0.8 c. You don't see the ball at 1.4 c — you see it at 0.946 c. Four animated cases show how velocities really add (and subtract) at relativistic speeds.

Thermal Radiation — Why Hot Things Glow

Stefan–Boltzmann, Wien's displacement law, and Planck's blackbody curve. Push the temperature slider and watch a rock glow red-hot to blue-white. See why the classical Rayleigh–Jeans curve blew up and forced Planck to invent quanta.

The Photoelectric Effect

Shine light on a metal cathode, tune the frequency and intensity, switch between materials from cesium to gold, and read off the stopping voltage on the I–V curve.

Double-Slit & Multi-Slit Interference

Three demos in one: vary λ and slit separation d, vary slit width a, or vary the number of slits N from 2 to 20 (a diffraction grating). Top-down view shows the wave field; sideways graph shows the screen-intensity pattern with the m-th fringe positions marked.

Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

Three demos: shrink a single slit and watch the diffraction pattern spread (position–momentum tradeoff in real photons), drag a Gaussian wavepacket and see its momentum-space twin breathe inversely, or shorten an excited-state lifetime to broaden the spectral line (energy–time uncertainty).

Davisson–Germer & Bragg — Electron Diffraction

Interactive textbook D–G apparatus: drag the movable detector along the arc and watch the I(θ) plot light up around the famous 50° peak (with a "classical, no-peak" comparison curve). Plus a Bragg-plane scene with a draggable θ slider that flips between constructive and destructive orders.

The Bohr Model — Atomic Energy Levels

Tune a laser onto an electron and watch it jump up an orbit, then drop back down emitting a photon of just the right wavelength. Switch atoms (H, He⁺, Li²⁺) to see how the spectral lines slide with Z². Built-in transition table highlights the active line in real time.

Particle in a Box — 1D, 2D & 3D

The simplest quantum bound state: a particle locked between two infinitely tall walls. Slide the quantum number n and watch ψ grow extra nodes; |ψ|² shows where the particle is most likely to be found. Three tabs cover 1-D, 2-D, and 3-D boxes — including degeneracy and 3-D slice planes.

Atomic Orbitals & Electron Configuration

The four quantum numbers (n, ℓ, m_ℓ, m_s), the s/p/d/f/g orbital shapes, the Aufbau filling order, and Hund's and Pauli's rules — all on one page. Includes an interactive periodic table where every element shows its full configuration, noble-gas shorthand, box diagram, and energy-level diagram.
