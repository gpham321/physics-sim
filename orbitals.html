Atomic Orbitals & Electron Configuration

Where electrons live, what their addresses look like, and how to write down the electron configuration of any element on the periodic table.

What is an atomic orbital?

An atomic orbital is a 3-D region around the nucleus where an electron is most likely to be found. It is not a planet-like orbit — the electron doesn't trace out a circle. Instead, the orbital is a probability cloud described by a wavefunction \(\psi(x,y,z)\), and \(|\psi|^2\) tells you how likely you are to find the electron at any given point.

Each orbital can hold at most two electrons (one spin-up, one spin-down — that's the Pauli exclusion principle). Orbitals come in different shapes and sizes, and we label each one with a set of four numbers called quantum numbers.

Address analogy. Think of the four quantum numbers as the electron's "address":
  • \(n\) — which floor of the building (energy level / shell)
  • \(\ell\) — which wing on that floor (subshell shape)
  • \(m_\ell\) — which apartment in that wing (orbital orientation)
  • \(m_s\) — which of the two beds in the apartment (spin direction)
No two electrons in the same atom can share all four numbers.

Counting orbitals and electrons

Once you fix \(n\) and \(\ell\), the number of orbitals in that subshell is \(2\ell + 1\) (one for each value of \(m_\ell\)), and each orbital holds 2 electrons (Pauli), so each subshell holds \(2(2\ell+1) = 4\ell + 2\) electrons.

Going deeper — what each quantum number actually means

Each of these numbers labels a specific physical property of the electron. Here's what's actually going on with each one — especially "orbital orientation" and "spin direction," which sound abstract but have very concrete meanings.

n — principal quantum number (energy level / shell)

Physical meaning: sets the electron's overall energy and roughly how far from the nucleus it sits, on average. Bigger \(n\) → higher energy → larger average distance.

Allowed values: any positive integer, \(n = 1, 2, 3, 4, \dots\)

Mental picture: floors of a building. Floor 1 (\(n=1\), the \(K\) shell) is closest to the nucleus and lowest energy; each higher floor is farther out and higher energy. Each floor has multiple "rooms" (subshells), labeled by \(\ell\).

For hydrogen (one electron), the energy depends only on \(n\): \(E_n = -13.6\,\text{eV}/n^2\). For multi-electron atoms it depends on both \(n\) and \(\ell\), which is why \(4s\) (\(n=4, \ell=0\)) actually fills before \(3d\) (\(n=3, \ell=2\)).

ℓ — angular momentum quantum number (subshell shape)

Physical meaning: how much orbital angular momentum the electron carries — i.e. how vigorously it is "swirling around" the nucleus. \(\ell = 0\) means none at all (so the orbital is a featureless sphere); larger \(\ell\) means more swirl, which produces directional lobes.

Allowed values: \(\ell = 0, 1, 2, \dots, n-1\). Each shell \(n\) has \(n\) possible subshells.

Letter codes: \(\ell = 0 \to s\), \(1 \to p\), \(2 \to d\), \(3 \to f\), \(4 \to g\). The letters are leftover from old spectroscopy — sharp, principal, diffuse, fundamental — and have stuck.

The shape of the orbital (sphere, dumbbell, cloverleaf, …) follows directly from \(\ell\). The exact mathematical statement: the magnitude of the orbital angular momentum is \(L = \sqrt{\ell(\ell+1)}\,\hbar\). For our purposes, just remember: \(\ell\) sets the shape.

m_ℓ — magnetic quantum number (orbital orientation)

Physical meaning: within a subshell, the orbitals all have the same shape but can point in different directions in space. \(m_\ell\) is the label that tells them apart.

Allowed values: \(m_\ell = -\ell, -\ell+1, \dots, -1, 0, 1, \dots, \ell-1, \ell\) — a total of \(2\ell + 1\) values.

What "orbital orientation" actually means. Take the three \(p\) orbitals (\(\ell = 1\)). They all look like dumbbells, and they all have the same energy. The only difference is which axis the dumbbell points along — \(x\), \(y\), or \(z\):

Same shape, three perpendicular orientations, three values of \(m_\ell\). For the \(d\) subshell (\(\ell = 2\)) there are five orientations (\(m_\ell = -2, -1, 0, +1, +2\)), corresponding to the five \(d\) shapes (\(d_{xy}, d_{yz}, d_{xz}, d_{x^2-y^2}, d_{z^2}\)). For \(f\) there are seven, and so on.

Why "magnetic"? In an external magnetic field, orbitals with different \(m_\ell\) acquire slightly different energies — that's the Zeeman effect. With no magnetic field, all \(2\ell+1\) orientations have the same energy and you can't distinguish them. Switch on a magnetic field, and a single spectral line splits into \(2\ell+1\) lines, one per orientation. That's where the name comes from.

s — spin quantum number (intrinsic angular momentum)

Physical meaning: beyond moving through space, an electron has a built-in property called spin. Spin behaves mathematically like angular momentum, even though the electron is a point particle and isn't actually rotating. Spin is just a feature of what an electron is — like its mass or charge.

Allowed values: for an electron, \(s = 1/2\) — always. It's a fixed property; you can't change it. Different particles have different fixed values: photons have \(s = 1\), the Higgs boson has \(s = 0\), pions have \(s = 0\). Electrons, protons, and neutrons all have \(s = 1/2\) (they're all "spin-½ particles").

Because \(s\) is fixed for electrons, it usually drops out of the listed quantum numbers — only \(m_s\) varies. The "four quantum numbers" you'll be asked about are \(n\), \(\ell\), \(m_\ell\), and \(m_s\).

m_s — spin magnetic quantum number (spin direction)

Physical meaning: the \(z\)-component of the spin — i.e. which way the electron's intrinsic angular momentum vector is pointing along whatever axis you choose to measure.

Allowed values: \(m_s = +1/2\) ("spin up", ↑) or \(m_s = -1/2\) ("spin down", ↓). Only those two — there is no in-between.

What "spin direction" actually means. Picture the electron as a tiny bar magnet. The magnet has a north pole and a south pole, and the line connecting them defines a direction. That direction is the spin direction.

Crucially: when you measure the electron's spin along any axis you choose, you only ever get one of those two outcomes — never "tilted at 45°," never "halfway up." This discreteness is one of the most non-classical things in physics. A classical bar magnet could point any direction; the electron's spin can only be parallel or anti-parallel to the measurement axis.

Spin direction is what doubles every orbital's capacity. Each spatial orbital — one combination of \((n, \ell, m_\ell)\) — holds two electrons, one with each spin direction. That's the Pauli exclusion principle: two electrons in the same orbital must have opposite spins so that all four quantum numbers don't match.

The first experimental evidence for spin came from the Stern–Gerlach experiment (1922): a beam of silver atoms passed through a non-uniform magnetic field and split into exactly two beams, not a continuous spread. Two beams = two values of \(m_s\) = quantized spin. Classical physics predicted a continuous spray; reality gave a sharp split. That was the first hard evidence that angular momentum at the atomic scale comes in fundamentally discrete packets.

The four-number summary, with physical meaning.
  • \(n\) sets the electron's energy and average distance from the nucleus.
  • \(\ell\) sets the orbital's shape (sphere, dumbbell, cloverleaf, …).
  • \(m_\ell\) sets the orbital's orientation in space (which axis the lobes point along).
  • \(m_s\) sets the electron's intrinsic spin direction (up or down along z).
No two electrons in the same atom can share all four — that's why each orbital holds at most two electrons, and why everything in chemistry from periodicity to bonding flows from this single rule.

The shapes of s, p, d, f, g orbitals

These are 2-D cross-section schematics of the actual 3-D orbitals. Cyan and red lobes mark regions where the wavefunction \(\psi\) has opposite sign — but remember, the probability density is \(|\psi|^2\), so both colors are equally likely places to find the electron. The sign matters for chemistry (it tells you how orbitals add when atoms bond), not for "where is the electron."

Quick mnemonic. The number of angular nodes (flat planes where \(\psi=0\)) equals \(\ell\). \(s\) has 0, \(p\) has 1, \(d\) has 2, \(f\) has 3, \(g\) has 4. The number of radial nodes (spherical shells where \(\psi=0\)) is \(n - \ell - 1\). So a \(2s\) orbital has 1 radial node and 0 angular nodes; a \(3p\) has 1 radial and 1 angular; a \(3d\) has 0 radial and 2 angular.

How electrons fill orbitals — three rules

1. Aufbau principle

Electrons fill the lowest-energy orbital first. We work from the bottom up.

2. Pauli exclusion principle

No two electrons in the same atom can have all four quantum numbers identical. Practically: each orbital holds at most two electrons, and they must have opposite spins (one \(m_s = +½\), one \(-½\)).

3. Hund's rule

When filling a subshell with multiple equal-energy orbitals (like the three \(p\) orbitals), put one electron in each empty orbital — all with parallel spins — before any orbital gets a second electron. Singly-occupied first, then double up.

Madelung's rule — the order of filling

The orbitals don't fill in the order \(1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 3d, 4s\dots\) — instead, \(4s\) sneaks in before \(3d\). The pattern is captured by the Madelung rule (also called the \((n+\ell)\) rule):

Fill subshells in order of increasing \(n + \ell\). For ties, fill the smaller \(n\) first.

The standard sequence the rule produces is:

1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d → 6p → 7s → 5f → 6d → 7p

Why this matters

  • Chemistry rests on the outermost shell. Two elements with the same valence-electron count (like all the alkali metals: 1 valence \(s\) electron) react in nearly identical ways. The whole periodic table is just a graphical bookkeeping device for orbital filling.
  • Color and light come from energy gaps. An atom emits light when an electron drops from one orbital to a lower one. The energy difference \(E_i - E_f\) sets the photon's frequency \(f = (E_i - E_f)/h\).
  • Spin makes magnetism. Atoms with unpaired electrons (Hund's rule!) act like tiny bar magnets — that's the origin of paramagnetism, ferromagnetism, and ultimately all magnetic technology from MRI to disk drives.
  • The shape of the periodic table is the shape of orbitals. The two-column \(s\)-block on the left, the six-column \(p\)-block on the right, the ten-column \(d\)-block in the middle, the fourteen-column \(f\)-block at the bottom — those numbers (\(2, 6, 10, 14\)) are the capacities \(2(2\ell+1)\) for \(\ell = 0, 1, 2, 3\). The periodic table is literally a map of orbital subshells.
